Promoting Emotional Intelligence in Preschool Children

Emotional intelligence can be taught. Laying that foundation in the preschool years gives children the beginning of emotional literacy — a head start with profound impact on lifelong happiness.

Get in Touch

In-service training for preschool teachers guides them to promote emotional intelligence in the classroom. Susan Gann offers such training, born of her deep experience both in preschool education and pediatric mental health.

Parents also benefit from this teaching as they learn to work with the emotional underpinnings of their child's behavior. Susan's long history of parent counseling and easy demeanor with parent groups makes her an ideal presenter on this subject. Her passion on this topic is compelling.

Together, we can build a generation of children with good mental health and self-esteem. While it's never too late to develop emotional intelligence in our children or ourselves, the wisdom of starting when kids are young is clear. Once the child enters grade school, emotional imbalances become viewed as behavior problems to be corrected. Let's help our children manage their emotional lives before that can happen.

Why Preschoolers

Little Kids, Big Feelings: The Preschool Years

Preschool years are dominated by big feelings: tantrums, tears, exuberance, seemingly unreasonable fears, freezing, the joy of mastery and more. Little kids often feel swamped by all this because their immature brains haven't yet developed the capacity for reasoning.

They experience their emotions as powerful body sensations and act them out through behavior, yielding the meltdowns that are so frequent in this age group. They need our help to manage their emotional fluctuations. When done well, this adult guidance lays the foundation that allows emotional intelligence to flourish.

The Foundation

What Is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional intelligence is the ability to correctly identify and understand one's own emotions and the emotions of others, then manage those feelings in a highly effective and adaptive manner. Unlike intellectual capacity (IQ), which is fixed at birth, emotional intelligence (EQ) can be learned and continues to develop throughout life.

Why Is Emotional Intelligence Important?

Numerous studies have shown that good emotional intelligence is the highest predictor of life satisfaction and adult wellbeing, exceeding even the satisfaction of career or relationship success. High levels of emotional intelligence correlate to a fulfilled and happy life, both personally and professionally.

This healthy management of feelings involves a number of skills that, collectively, are referred to as emotional self-regulation. It begins with high levels of self-awareness, recognizing how emotions drive thoughts and behavior in themselves and others, and how emotions impact the state of our nervous systems. People with good emotional intelligence know how to sort out what's going on and calm themselves when emotionally triggered. They are less reactive because they are able to think through how they wish to respond in an emotionally charged situation. They recognize the impact of their emotions on other people. They have the ability to understand someone else's perspective and so develop genuine compassion and empathy for others. All this leads to good social skills and interpersonal relationships that are largely gratifying. These folks are good at conflict management, stress tolerance and stress reduction. They are open to others rather than defensive. They are adaptable and resilient. They have good self-esteem and their overall outlook is optimistic.

The Practice

How Can This Be Taught in Preschool and at Home?

It begins with teachers and parents recognizing that all emotional outbursts have a trigger. "Bad" behavior in kids is almost never because a child wants to create a problem, but rather because they don't know how else to manage. The supervising adult needs to mentally pause in response to this behavior and ask themselves what has prompted it. This allows the adult to shift into coregulation with the child, the first step in helping kids manage overwhelming emotions.

  1. 1

    What Is Coregulation?

    Coregulation is the act of the adult essentially lending their sense of calm to the child. It occurs when the adult lets go of their own reactivity and connects with the child from a place of calm neutrality and respect. It can look as simple and passive as just sitting near a tantruming child, maintaining one's own peaceful composure until that calm rubs off on the child. It is often — especially as the child matures — more active, with verbal expressions of empathy and the naming of the emotion driving the behavior.

  2. 2

    Name It to Tame It

    Naming the emotion helps the child begin to understand what's going on when they're melting down. Statements like "You're really angry" or "That was scary" are validating and supportive and help an out-of-control child begin to shift.

  3. 3

    Next Steps in Coregulation

    After naming the feeling, expressions of empathy help enormously. This can sound like "I know that's hard. I get angry sometimes too." Children assume their experiences and feelings are unique to them. It is incredibly reassuring for them to hear that others share similar emotional experiences and can manage them without overwhelm. This is where emotional self-regulation — the key to emotional intelligence — begins.

  4. 4

    Teaching Emotional Management Skills

    There are many practical tools children can be taught to deal with big emotions, but such teaching can only happen when calm is restored. Teaching these skills needs to happen outside of the blow-up, because a child having an emotional meltdown is a child whose immature brain has been hijacked by overwhelming sensations. A child in that state is incapable of reasoning or learning new skills. When teachers and parents consciously teach emotional management skills as a regular part of a child's day, they give kids tools to employ when big feelings hit.

  5. 5

    What Are Emotional Management Skills?

    Emotionally intelligent people have a wide variety of tools they employ to manage overwhelming emotions, many of which can be taught to young children. These include numerous techniques for focusing on and directing the breath, the release of excess physical energy brought on by excess/difficult emotions, singing and humming, specific physical movements to restore a sense of agency, and others. Recognizing the emotional cues of those around us builds on these skills by instilling empathy.

    Preschool teachers and parents promote emotional intelligence in young children by incorporating such teaching in their interactions with kids. Sometimes this teaching happens as a game; sometimes it happens through casual comments and observations; sometimes it happens by reflecting on the emotional experiences of characters in the books we read to them. Teachers and parents who consciously employ this approach lay vital groundwork for the lifelong wellbeing of children in our care.

About

Susan C. Gann, Parent/Teacher Trainer

Susan is passionate about building emotional intelligence in young children, having spent much of her adult life in that pursuit. She began her professional career with a Master's in Early Childhood Education, working as a preschool teacher and program director for 7 years. She then returned to graduate school, obtaining a Master's of Mental Health from the Johns Hopkins School of Medicine. Utilizing this combined training, she worked as a child psychotherapist and parent counselor for 20 years before moving to California. Susan no longer works as a therapist but has continued to expand her skills and understanding of children and human nature in general. She became a Certified Instructor of Infant Massage, working with parents and their babies on how to build deep connection and comfort. She pursued training in energy medicine, becoming a Reiki master, certified acupressurist, and practitioner of Healing Touch. This devotion to the healing arts brought her back to her foundation: working with children and parents to build positive mental health. Along the way, this journey has taught her the importance of emotional intelligence as her own has flourished.

Susan currently serves as Parent Advisor to the New School of Berkeley, a respected and well-established preschool in the San Francisco East Bay area. She also serves on New School's Board of Directors and has provided emotional intelligence in-service training to New School staff. She is available to provide similar training to parents and other preschools in the community.
